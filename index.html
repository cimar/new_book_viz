Bias, She Wrote

By Rosie Cima

How much progress has American literature really made towards gender equality?

Shirley Jackson was one of the literary superstars of the 1940s, 50s and 60s. Today her work is considered classic, but she was also celebrated in her lifetime. She wrote 17 books, sold her writing to the top magazines and publishing houses in the country, won awards, and had her work adapted for television, film, and the ballet.

Jackson was also a woman, wife, and mother. In the 1950s, this came with an obvious downside. When Jackson went into the hospital to deliver her third child, she had the following exchange with the receptionist filling out her form:

"Occupation?"
"Writer," I said.
"Housewife," she said.
"Writer," I said.
"I'll just put down housewife," she said.

It certainly seems like gender equality in literature has come a long way in the past seventy years. For one thing: if a woman says she's a writer, I think people have an easier time accepting that she's a god damned writer.

But then again, I'm a writer and a woman. And I'm an optimist. So I could be wrong.

How much progress has American literature actually made towards gender equality in the past 70 years?

To answer this question, I looked at the gender of the authors of books on the New York Times Best Sellers List over time.

The list consists of the 10-15 best-selling books in America, and has been published weekly since the 1940s. Making it onto the list is a good indicator of a book’s commercial success -- the list is based off of surveys of booksellers -- but it’s also a marketing boon on its own, as people often tend to discover or decide to purchase books because they've appeared on the list.

Books by women composed 25-30% of the list in the 1950s. The 1960s and the 1970s were volatile. This probalby has something to do with the Women’s Liberation movement, which was in full swing at the time. But the list was also temporarily capped at 10 books a week -- making the annual list of books shorter, and more sensitive to small changes. Female representation reached a local high of 38% in 1970 and a low of 14% in 1975.

But after the bras had burned and the 10-book limit was lifted, not much had changed: when the 1980s came to a close, 28% of the books on the list were written by women. Lasting change didn’t come until the 1990s, when female writers made steady gains onto the list until 2001. After that, growth flattened and stayed flat, just beneath the 50% line.
